The Plan

Play it smart · 48 hours in Tokyo

A menu, not a schedule. A few things reward the sequence. Tap + on anything worth remembering.

Do this the moment you arrive
  • Buy a Suica card at any station machine. Load 3,000 yen. It taps in and out of every train, subway, and bus in the city, and works at convenience stores and vending machines. The single thing that makes Tokyo frictionless.
  • Check in early if you can. Ebisu Garden Place is your anchor. Daikanyama is a 10-minute walk, Nakameguro is 15, Shibuya is 15 on foot or one JR stop.
Build the two days around these
  • Wednesday morning: start at Onibus Coffee in Nakameguro, walk the canal south into Daikanyama, and end at T-Site. That stretch alone is most of a good Wednesday, and it all connects on foot with no transit.
  • Ichiran: do it for lunch or an early dinner. Shibuya branch is 15 min on foot from Ebisu. Go before noon or after 2pm to skip the queue. No reservation, no group needed.
  • Hambagu dinner: Grill Murayama in Ginza for the classic yoshoku set. It's 25 min by train from Ebisu; go Wednesday evening before 6pm to get a counter seat without waiting.
Thursday: watch the clock
  • Leave the hotel no later than 2:30 PM. JR Yamanote Line from Ebisu to Hamamatsuchō (~16 min), then Tokyo Monorail direct to Haneda Terminal 3 (~21 min). Total ~37 min, about $4. Monorail runs every 10 min. Confirm your terminal before heading out.
  • Thursday morning is one thing, not two. Yanaka is 35 min by train and the right call if you leave by 9am and head back by noon. Shimokitazawa is 30 min and looser. Pick one.

Eat

Ranked. A solo ramen booth by design, the yoshoku hambagu set that's been on the menu for 70 years, the lunch counter you find on the way to somewhere else.

1Ichiran

~$12–16
★ Top three · the solo bowl
hakata tonkotsuShibuya · 15 min walkwalk-in, no res

Built specifically for solo dining: a wooden booth with a bamboo curtain, a flavor form to fill out (broth richness, noodle firmness, garlic level, spice), and a bowl of Hakata tonkotsu that appears in front of you without a word exchanged. No shared tables, no awkward timing, no eye contact required. When the bowl gets low, ask for kaedama (extra noodles) and they arrive in 30 seconds. The Shibuya location at Udagawacho is the closest to Ebisu, a 15-minute walk through the backstreets. Lines move fast even when they look long.

MapSite

2Rengatei

~$15–25
★ Top three · the hambagu
yoshoku since 1895Ginza · 25 min by trainwalk-in (lunch fills fast)

The original Tokyo yoshoku restaurant, founded in 1895, and the birthplace of several dishes that became Japanese staples. The hambagu teishoku is what "hamburger rice" actually means in Japan: a thick hand-formed beef patty, demi-glace sauce, rice, miso, pickles, a small salad, at an old wooden counter. The menu has barely moved in a century and the kitchen is very good at the thing it's always done. Go Wednesday evening; the lunch service is packed but the dinner hour is calmer. Ginza 3-chome, 3 minutes from Ginza Station.

3Any Teishoku Near You

~$8–13
★ Top three · the real lunch
set lunchEbisu / Daikanyamawalk-in

The most underrated meal in Japan: a lunch set of grilled fish or chicken karaage or saba (mackerel) with rice, miso, pickles, and a small salad, under 1,200 yen, eaten at a counter. Every neighborhood in Ebisu and Daikanyama has two or three. Look for the handwritten lunch boards out front, or the plastic food display cases at street level. Don't overthink it. The fish of the day at a counter seat near the canal is often the best meal on the whole trip.

4AFURI Ebisu

~$10–14
yuzu shio ramenEbisu · 2 min walk from stationwalk-in

The ramen that's walking distance from the hotel. AFURI's signature is a clean yuzu-citrus shio (salt) broth, lighter and more aromatic than tonkotsu, with a char-siu that's grilled at the table-side counter. Completely different from Ichiran: open, social, and fast. Good for a quick lunch or early dinner when you don't want to go far. The Ebisu branch is right by the station on the Daikanyama side.

4The Konbini

~$3–7
7-Eleven / Lawson / FamilyMarteverywhere

Mentioned seriously, not as a fallback. Japanese convenience store food is a genuine category: onigiri with seasonal fillings, egg-salad sandwiches on milk bread, hot oden by the pot, fresh katsu sando, steamed bao, and strong canned coffee. Pick up breakfast here and eat it on the canal. It's as good as most cafes. 7-Eleven is the most consistent; Lawson has the better desserts.

Coffee

Ranked. Tokyo takes coffee more seriously than almost anywhere. Two of the best spots in the city are steps from the canal.

1Onibus Coffee

~$4–6
★ Top three
specialty roasterNakameguro · 15 min walkwalk-in, opens 9am

A small roaster in a converted house steps from the Nakameguro canal: seasonal espresso, serious filter, and a quiet room with natural light. The right first stop before the canal gets crowded. Ask what's on filter, sit by the window, take as long as you want. The size of a living room, which is the whole point. Walk here first, then north along the canal.

~$8–14
★ Top three · the spectacle
four-floor canal-side set pieceNakameguro · 15 min walkwalk-in

Worth the visit even if you've been to one of the other locations. Do

Ranked. A bookstore that's its own destination, the most famous intersection in the world, a canal walk that earns the afternoon.

1Daikanyama T-Site

free
★ Top three
magazine library + vinyl + cafeDaikanyama · 12 min walkwalk-in, opens 7am

What a bookstore looks like when it's done right. A three-building mid-century compound: a magazine library stocking publications from 30+ countries, a vinyl lounge with listening stations, a photo book section that runs floor to ceiling, and the Anjin cafe where you sit with a rare magazine and let the morning go. Designed by Klein Dytham Architecture. Go before 11am for the quiet version. No pressure to buy anything; the building is the point. One of the best places to spend an hour in Tokyo, and it's a 12-minute walk from Ebisu.

MapSite

2Nakameguro Canal Walk

free
★ Top three · the anchor walk
meguro river + cherry trees15 min walk from Ebisuany time

The Meguro River runs about 2km through Nakameguro, both banks lined with restaurants and cafes that put terraces down to the water, cherry trees overhead, and footbridges at each block. Beautiful in any season. Start at the south end near Ebisu and walk north toward Nakameguro Station, stopping at Onibus and the Roastery along the way. The full loop back through Daikanyama to T-Site is 3 to 4 hours if you let it be, which you should on Wednesday.

3Shibuya Scramble Crossing

free
★ Top three
the intersectionShibuya · 15 min walkany time

The most photographed intersection in the world, and it earns it on foot: when the light turns and up to 3,000 people converge from six directions at once, it's genuinely disorienting in the best way. Cross it a few times. Best overhead view is from the second-floor Starbucks in the Q-Front building on the northeast corner, looking straight down. Go at night for the full neon-and-crowd version. Combines naturally with Ichiran, which is a 5-minute walk from the crossing into the Udagawacho backstreets.

4Miyashita Park

free
rooftop skate park + viewsShibuya · 15 min walkwalk-in

A former city park rebuilt as a four-level rooftop complex: a skate park on the top level with views over Shibuya, boutiques and a good coffee shop below. Not a tourist sight, an actual working neighborhood park that happens to be on a building. Combine it with Ichiran, which is directly across the street. The roof at dusk is the Shibuya view that isn't the crossing.

Wander

Ranked. A triangle of the best walkable neighborhoods in Tokyo, a bohemian pocket of record shops and live music, old Tokyo where very little has changed.

1Ebisu / Daikanyama / Nakameguro

free
★ Top three · home territory
the walkable triangleall on foot from base

Three connected neighborhoods that form the best walkable cluster in Tokyo. Ebisu has the Garden Place brick complex and a quiet covered shopping street. Walk 10 minutes west into Daikanyama and the boutiques get smaller, better, and less branded. Keep walking 5 minutes into Nakameguro and you're on the canal. The whole triangle is flat and interconnected, no transit needed. Best in the order: Ebisu to Daikanyama to Nakameguro and back. This is the default Wednesday plan.

2Shimokitazawa

free
★ Top three
vintage + live music + record shopsSetagaya · 30 minwalk-in

Tokyo's bohemian pocket: narrow lanes, vintage record and clothing stores, live music venues every 20 meters, and a young crowd that lives there rather than visits. Flash Disc Ranch for vinyl, Bear Pond Espresso for coffee, and an afternoon where you wander without a destination. Take the Tokyu Inokashira Line from Shibuya two stops. Go on a weekday when it's at its most local. There's no single thing to do here; the neighborhood is the thing.

Map

3Yanaka

free
★ Top three · old Tokyo
pre-war Tokyo intactTaito-ku · 35 min by trainThu AM

The Tokyo that didn't get flattened in the war or the redevelopment that followed: narrow alleys, old wooden machiya houses, temples tucked between them, a cemetery locals walk through like a park, and Yanaka Ginza, a covered shotengai where vendors have been selling sembei and fresh tofu for decades. Go before noon on Thursday when it's at its quietest. Combine with a 10-minute walk to Nezu Shrine if the timing works. Take the JR Yamanote Line to Nippori.

Map

4Omotesando

free
architecture walkShibuya · 20 min walkwalk-in

Skip Takeshita-dori (tourist noise) and walk Omotesando proper: a broad boulevard of flagship stores designed by Tadao Ando, Toyo Ito, and Herzog and de Meuron. The Prada building is a glass diamond. The Omotesando Hills mall is a Tadao Ando spiral cut into a hillside. Turn into Cat Street for smaller shops and less crowd. The architecture is the reason; this is a good Shibuya add-on after the crossing and Ichiran.

Tips

The handful of things that make Tokyo easy. Read once, then move.

Suica card: buy at any station on arrival, load 3,000 yen. Taps in and out of every train, subway, and bus. Works at konbini and vending machines. Top up at any station machine in 30 seconds. This is the move.

Getting around: Google Maps works perfectly here, transit directions accurate to the minute. Taxis exist but are expensive ($25+ across town); the subway is faster. Ebisu to Daikanyama is 12 min on foot. Ebisu to Shibuya is 15 min on foot or one JR stop. More walkable than it looks on a map.

Haneda from Ebisu: JR Yamanote Line from Ebisu to Hamamatsuchō (~16 min), then Tokyo Monorail direct to Haneda Terminal 3 (~21 min). Total ~37 min, about $4. Monorail runs every 10 minutes. Leave the hotel by 2:30 PM for a 4:30 PM flight. Confirm your terminal (international = Terminal 3) before you head out.

Cash: carry 5,000–10,000 yen. Most restaurants, cafes, and shops take cards. Vending machines, some small teishoku counters, and street vendors are cash only. 7-Eleven ATMs accept all foreign cards, no fee.

No tipping. Anywhere. Not in restaurants, not in taxis, not at hotels. It's not expected and the gesture can confuse. The price is the price.

Trains: phone on silent, calls taken off the train, voice low. Not a rule, just what everyone does. The quiet is part of the experience.

Convenience stores: 7-Eleven, Lawson, and FamilyMart are legitimately good food, not emergencies. The egg-salad sando, seasonal onigiri, and hot oden are worth seeking out. Coffee from the machine at the register is better than most places charging $7 for it.

Ichiran queue: go before noon or after 2pm. The lunch and dinner rushes add 20–30 min of outdoor queue. The bowl is identical at 11am and 7pm.

T-Site hours: opens 7am daily, quietest before 11am. The vinyl lounge and magazine library are on the upper floors of the left and right buildings; don't just walk into the ground-floor retail and leave thinking that's it.
