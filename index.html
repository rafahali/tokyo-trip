The Plan

Play it smart · 48 hours in Tokyo

A menu, not a schedule. Plain English throughout — no assumed Japan knowledge. Tap + on anything worth remembering.

Do this the moment you arrive
  • Get a Suica card at any train station machine (look for the IC card / Suica option on the screen). It's a rechargeable tap card, like a MetroCard, but it works on every train, subway, and bus in the city AND at convenience stores and vending machines. Load 3,000 yen (~$20). You'll tap it constantly. Without it, buying individual tickets every time is a headache.
  • Your base is Ebisu. Think of it as the West Village of Tokyo: residential, walkable, not touristy. From there, Daikanyama (10 min walk) is like Nolita — small independent boutiques, quieter streets. Nakameguro (15 min walk) is the canal neighborhood, a river lined with coffee shops and restaurants. Shibuya (15 min walk or one train stop) is the big commercial hub with the famous crossing.
What you're actually doing and eating
  • Wednesday morning walk: Start at Onibus Coffee (a specialty roaster, like a serious Brooklyn coffee shop) in Nakameguro. Walk the canal south into Daikanyama. Stop at Kyu Asakura House — a 1919 wooden Japanese residence preserved exactly as it was, tucked between boutiques, costs almost nothing to enter. End at T-Site: a bookstore, magazine archive, and vinyl listening room in a connected set of mid-century buildings. Go upstairs — that's where the rare magazines and listening stations are.
  • Ichiran (if the booth format appeals): Ramen is Japanese noodle soup. Ichiran's version is tonkotsu — a rich, creamy pork-bone broth with thin wheat noodles. What makes Ichiran different: the whole restaurant is designed for solo dining. You sit alone in a wooden booth, fill out a paper form to customize everything (broth richness, noodle firmness, garlic, spice level), and the bowl arrives through a bamboo curtain in front of you. Zero awkward group seating. ~$12–16. Worth knowing: everyone who has been to Tokyo more than once will tell you the food is fine but the city has much better ramen. Go for the format, not the bowl. If you want actual great ramen nearby, AFURI Ebisu (in the guide) is 2 minutes from base and worth the bowl specifically.
  • Wednesday afternoon, Shibuya: From T-Site, walk or take one train stop to Shibuya. Scramble Crossing is 15 minutes on foot. Miyashita Park is directly across from Ichiran. If the solo booth appeals, do Ichiran here for a late lunch (go before 2pm to avoid the rush).
  • Wednesday evening, the canal: Back toward the base. Kushiwaekamaru is Michelle's pick: canal-side yakitori around 7pm, walk home along the river after. If you want to splurge instead, Yoroniku (5 min from the hotel, reservation needed) is the highest-signal dinner rec in the guide. Pick one — these are the same evening.
Thursday: watch the clock
  • Thursday morning is one neighborhood. Yanaka is old Tokyo: pre-war wooden houses, narrow alleys, a covered market street (35 min by train, JR to Nippori). Shimokitazawa is Tokyo's bohemian neighborhood: vintage, record shops, live houses (30 min from Shibuya). Pick one, be back at the hotel by noon.
  • Rengatei for Thursday lunch — then straight to the airport. Ginza is 25 min by train from Ebisu. Eat at Rengatei around noon (the 130-year-old yoshoku restaurant, the hambagu teishoku). Done by 1pm, then 10 min by subway from Ginza to Hamamatsuchō for the monorail to Haneda. Total transit: about 35 min. This is the cleanest possible Thursday. Skip Rengatei if you want a slower morning.
  • Hard out by 2:30 PM. JR Yamanote from Ebisu to Hamamatsuchō (~16 min), then Tokyo Monorail direct to Haneda Terminal 3 (~21 min). Total ~37 min, about $4. Monorail runs every 10 min. International = Terminal 3 — confirm before you leave.
Book Before You Go
☐ Hotel — Westin Tokyo (Ebisu Garden Place) or Trunk Hotel (Daikanyama). Book now; Ebisu fills fast.
☐ Yoroniku — High-end yakiniku, reservation recommended. Check tableall.com or their site.
☐ Kushiwaekamaru — Walk-in, but verify current status on Tabelog before going.

Eat

Recs from Michelle at the top. Ichiran for the booth, not the bowl. The canal-side yakitori and the yoshoku hambagu that's been on the menu since 1895 are the food recs.

1Ichiran

~$12–16
★ Top three · the solo booth
hakata tonkotsuShibuya · 15 min walkwalk-in, no res

Built specifically for solo dining: a wooden booth with a bamboo curtain, a flavor form to fill out (broth richness, noodle firmness, garlic level, spice), and a bowl of Hakata tonkotsu that appears in front of you without a word exchanged. No shared tables, no awkward timing, no eye contact required. When the bowl gets low, ask for kaedama (extra noodles) and they arrive in 30 seconds. Go for the format. Everyone who's been to Tokyo multiple times says the same thing: the food is fine, the city has much better ramen. If you want the ramen to be the point, AFURI Ebisu is in this guide, a 2-minute walk from the hotel, and worth the bowl specifically. The Shibuya location at Udagawacho is closest to Ebisu, a 15-minute walk through the backstreets. Lines move fast even when they look long.

2Kushiwaekamaru

~$20–35
★ Top three · Michelle rec · canal-side yakitori
kushiyaki over binchōtanKamimeguro · 15 min walkwalk-inFriend RecWed evening

Michelle's pick for a Wednesday evening: a skewer counter right on the Kamimeguro stretch of the canal, grilling yakitori and kushiyaki over white charcoal while the water runs outside. No independent Reddit signal, but Michelle is a credible source and this is exactly the type of spot that hasn't made it to the algorithm yet. The canal path back to Ebisu on foot is 15 minutes; the right way to end the evening is to walk it. Go around 7pm. Verified fallback if this doesn't pan out: Toriyoshi, 500m north on the same canal, three independent specialist endorsements. Walk-in only, arrive 20 minutes before opening. Foreigners are served a la carte, not omakase.

3Rengatei

~$15–25
★ Top three · the hambagu
yoshoku since 1895Ginza · 25 min by trainwalk-in (arrive by noon)Thu lunch

The original Tokyo yoshoku restaurant, founded in 1895, and the birthplace of several dishes that became Japanese staples. The hambagu teishoku is what "hamburger rice" actually means in Japan: a thick hand-formed beef patty, demi-glace sauce, rice, miso, pickles, a small salad, at an old wooden counter. The menu has barely moved in a century. Go Thursday for lunch around noon: done by 1pm, then Ginza to Hamamatsuchō is 10 min by subway for the Haneda monorail. The cleanest way to end the trip.

4Any Teishoku Near You

~$8–13
set lunchEbisu / Daikanyamawalk-in

The most underrated meal in Japan: a lunch set of grilled fish or chicken karaage or saba (mackerel) with rice, miso, pickles, and a small salad, under 1,200 yen, eaten at a counter. Every neighborhood in Ebisu and Daikanyama has two or three. Look for the handwritten lunch boards out front, or the plastic food display cases at street level. Don't overthink it. The fish of the day at a counter seat near the canal is often the best meal on the whole trip.

5AFURI Ebisu

~$10–14
yuzu shio ramenEbisu · 2 min walk from stationwalk-in

The ramen that's walking distance from the base hotel, and the one to go to if you want the food to actually matter. AFURI's signature is a clean yuzu-citrus shio (salt) broth, lighter and more aromatic than the rich tonkotsu at Ichiran, with a char-siu that's grilled at the counter. Completely different style: open, social, and fast. Good for a quick lunch or early dinner when you don't want to travel. The Ebisu branch is right by the station on the Daikanyama side.

The highest-signal food mention in the entire trip dataset: one OP who did 16 days across Japan said this was "one of the best meals I had in Japan, the silky beef will be a memory I keep forever." Yakiniku is tabletop Korean-style BBQ, beef here specifically, grilled on a small counter grill in front of you. Tabelog 4.3+. 8The Konbini

~$3–7
7-Eleven / Lawson / FamilyMarteverywhere

Mentioned seriously, not as a fallback. Japanese convenience store food is a genuine category: onigiri with seasonal fillings, egg-salad sandwiches on milk bread, hot oden by the pot, fresh katsu sando, steamed bao, and strong canned coffee. Pick up breakfast here and eat it on the canal. It's as good as most cafes. 7-Eleven is the most consistent; Lawson has the better desserts.

Coffee

Ranked. Tokyo takes specialty coffee more seriously than almost anywhere. Onibus and Woodberry are steps from the canal. Glitch is worth the detour if the cup is the point.

1Onibus Coffee

~$4–6
★ Top three
specialty roasterNakameguro · 15 min walkwalk-in, opens 9am

A small roaster in a converted house steps from the Nakameguro canal: seasonal espresso, serious filter, and a quiet room with natural light. The right first stop before the canal gets crowded. Ask what's on filter, sit by the window, take as long as you want. The size of a living room, which is the whole point. Walk here first, then north along the canal.

2Woodberry Coffee Roasters

~$4–6
★ Top three · the neighborhood pick
specialty roasterDaikanyama · 12 min walkwalk-in

The local's choice when Onibus has a line. A Daikanyama roaster with serious filter work and an unhurried room, in the same core neighborhood as T-Site and Kyu Asakura House. The crowd here skews local in a way that Onibus increasingly doesn't. Make it the second stop on the canal walk, or the first coffee if you're starting from Daikanyama.

3Glitch Coffee

~$5–15
★ Top three · the obsessive's detour
single origin filterJimbocho · 30 min by trainwalk-in, waits common

The highest-signal specialty coffee spot in Tokyo and a genuine detour from Ebisu. Jimbocho is the city's secondhand bookstore district, which makes the trip worth it on its own. Single-origin filter, rotating seasonal beans, Ethiopia naturals done exceptionally well. Pricing runs 650–2,000 yen depending on the bean. Expect a short wait; the brews are slow. Worth it if coffee is actually the point of the day.

Do

Ranked. A preserved Meiji-era house two minutes from T-Site, a bookstore that's its own destination, the most famous intersection in the world.

1Daikanyama T-Site

free
★ Top three
magazine library + vinyl + cafeDaikanyama · 12 min walkwalk-in, opens 7am

What a bookstore looks like when it's done right. A three-building mid-century compound: a magazine library stocking publications from 30+ countries, a vinyl lounge with listening stations, a photo book section that runs floor to ceiling, and the Anjin cafe where you sit with a rare magazine and let the morning go. Designed by Klein Dytham Architecture. Go before 11am for the quiet version. No pressure to buy anything; the building is the point. One of the best places to spend an hour in Tokyo, and it's a 12-minute walk from Ebisu. Kyu Asakura House is 2 minutes away on foot.

MapSite

2Kyu Asakura House

~$2 entry
★ Top three · Michelle rec
1919 Meiji-era residenceSarugakucho · 2 min from T-Sitewalk-inFriend Rec

A preserved 1919 wooden residence on the Sarugakucho lane that connects Daikanyama to the canal, designated a national cultural asset. Built in the Meiji style with a traditional garden, interior rooms you can walk through, and a rock garden that's been unchanged for over a century. One of the few intact traditional Japanese houses accessible in central Tokyo, and it's entirely surrounded by boutiques and coffee shops. Do it between T-Site and the canal walk; it's two minutes on foot and costs almost nothing.

3Nakameguro Canal Walk

free
★ Top three · the anchor walk
meguro river + cherry trees15 min walk from Ebisuany time

The Meguro River runs about 2km through Nakameguro, both banks lined with restaurants and cafes that put terraces down to the water, cherry trees overhead, and footbridges at each block. Beautiful in any season. Start at the south end near Ebisu and walk north toward Nakameguro Station, stopping at Onibus along the canal. The full loop back through Daikanyama to T-Site is 3 to 4 hours if you let it be, which you should on Wednesday. Kushiwaekamaru sits right on the canal for the evening if you want to finish the walk with dinner.

4Shibuya Scramble Crossing

free
the intersectionShibuya · 15 min walkany time

The most photographed intersection in the world, and it earns it on foot: when the light turns and up to 3,000 people converge from six directions at once, it's genuinely disorienting in the best way. Cross it a few times. Best overhead view is from the second-floor Starbucks in the Q-Front building on the northeast corner, looking straight down. Go at night for the full neon-and-crowd version. Combines naturally with Ichiran, which is a 5-minute walk from the crossing into the Udagawacho backstreets.

free
rooftop skate park + viewsShibuya · 15 min walkwalk-in

A former city park rebuilt as a four-level rooftop complex: a skate park on the top level with views over Shibuya, boutiques and a good coffee shop below. Wander

Ranked. The walkable triangle that is the best cluster in Tokyo, a bohemian pocket of record shops and live music, old Tokyo where very little has changed.

1Ebisu / Daikanyama / Nakameguro

free
★ Top three · home territory
the walkable triangleall on foot from base

Three connected neighborhoods that form the best walkable cluster in Tokyo, confirmed by Michelle: west-village energy, lots of cute boutiques, well-rated coffee everywhere, and the canal. Ebisu has the Garden Place brick complex and a quiet covered shopping street. Walk 10 minutes west into Daikanyama and the boutiques get smaller, better, and less branded. Keep walking 5 minutes into Nakameguro and you're on the canal. The whole triangle is flat and interconnected, no transit needed. Best in the order: Ebisu to Daikanyama to Nakameguro and back. This is the default Wednesday plan.

2Shimokitazawa

free
★ Top three
vintage + live music + record shopsSetagaya · 30 minwalk-in

Tokyo's bohemian pocket: narrow lanes, vintage record and clothing stores, live music venues every 20 meters, and a young crowd that lives there rather than visits. Flash Disc Ranch for vinyl, Bear Pond Espresso for coffee, and an afternoon where you wander without a destination. Take the Tokyu Inokashira Line from Shibuya two stops. Go on a weekday when it's at its most local. There's no single thing to do here; the neighborhood is the thing. The live houses here (Basement Bar, Three) are some of the warmest, most accessible music spots in the city for a solo night out.

3Yanaka

free
★ Top three · old Tokyo
pre-war Tokyo intactTaito-ku · 35 min by trainThu AM

The Tokyo that didn't get flattened in the war or the redevelopment that followed: narrow alleys, old wooden machiya houses, temples tucked between them, a cemetery locals walk through like a park, and Yanaka Ginza, a covered shotengai where vendors have been selling sembei and fresh tofu for decades. Go before noon on Thursday when it's at its quietest. Combine with a 10-minute walk to Nezu Shrine if the timing works. Take the JR Yamanote Line to Nippori.

4Golden Gai

~$5–15 per bar
200 tiny bars in one alleyShinjuku · 20 min by trainWed or Thu evening

The most social night out in Tokyo for a solo traveler: a Shinjuku alleyway of 200+ bars, most holding 8 to 12 people at most. The format forces conversation with whoever's next to you in a way almost nowhere else does. Each bar has a theme: jazz bars, film-poster bars, bars run by a single person who's been there 30 years. Door charges run 300–500 yen. The bars in the north alley skew slightly less tourist-forward. Take the Yamanote Line to Shinjuku, about 20 minutes from Ebisu.

5Omotesando

free
architecture walkShibuya · 20 min walkwalk-in

Skip Takeshita-dori (tourist noise) and walk Omotesando proper: a broad boulevard of flagship stores designed by Tadao Ando, Toyo Ito, and Herzog and de Meuron. The Prada building is a glass diamond. The Omotesando Hills mall is a Tadao Ando spiral cut into a hillside. Turn into Cat Street for smaller shops and less crowd. The architecture is the reason; this is a good Shibuya add-on after the crossing and Ichiran.

Tips

The handful of things that make Tokyo easy. Read once, then move.

Suica card: buy at any station on arrival, load 3,000 yen. Taps in and out of every train, subway, and bus. Works at konbini and vending machines. Top up at any station machine in 30 seconds. This is the move.

Getting around: Google Maps works perfectly here, transit directions accurate to the minute. Taxis exist but are expensive ($25+ across town); the subway is faster. Ebisu to Daikanyama is 12 min on foot. Ebisu to Shibuya is 15 min on foot or one JR stop. More walkable than it looks on a map.

Last train: trains stop between 11pm and midnight. Check Google Maps for the specific line you're on before a late night. Taxis are always available at major stations but expensive. The last Yamanote Line from Shinjuku toward Ebisu runs around 12:30am; don't cut it close.

Haneda from Ebisu: JR Yamanote Line from Ebisu to Hamamatsuchō (~16 min), then Tokyo Monorail direct to Haneda Terminal 3 (~21 min). Total ~37 min, about $4. Monorail runs every 10 minutes. Leave by 2:30 PM for a 4:30 PM flight. Confirm your terminal (international = Terminal 3) before you head out.

Cash: carry 5,000–10,000 yen. Most restaurants, cafes, and shops take cards. Vending machines, some small teishoku counters, and street vendors are cash only. 7-Eleven ATMs accept all foreign cards, no fee.

No tipping. Anywhere. Not in restaurants, not in taxis, not at hotels. It's not expected and the gesture can confuse. The price is the price.

Trains: phone on silent, calls taken off the train, voice low. Not a rule, just what everyone does. The quiet is part of the experience.

Kabukicho and Roppongi at night: safe to walk through but watch for street touts working host bars and girls bars. The pitch is always a drink; the bill is always a surprise. Ignore anyone who approaches you with an invitation. This is the only consistent financial scam in Tokyo and it's specific to these two areas. Stick to Golden Gai for the Shinjuku night.

Convenience stores: 7-Eleven, Lawson, and FamilyMart are legitimately good food, not emergencies. The egg-salad sando, seasonal onigiri, and hot oden are worth seeking out. Coffee from the machine at the register is better than most places charging $7 for it.

Ichiran queue: go before noon or after 2pm. The lunch and dinner rushes add 20–30 min of outdoor queue. The bowl is identical at 11am and 7pm.

T-Site hours: opens 7am daily, quietest before 11am. The vinyl lounge and magazine library are on the upper floors of the left and right buildings; don't just walk into the ground-floor retail and leave thinking that's it.
